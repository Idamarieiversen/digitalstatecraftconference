§ 05

About

From early e-government experiments in the 1990s to today’s AI-driven public services, states have long sought to harness digital technologies to transform how they interact with citizens. The 2010s saw many countries collaborate on “digital transformation,” building an international community around ideals of openness and participation, while simultaneously competing in global rankings and using digital initiatives as instruments of nation branding and statecraft.

These dynamics unfold within a highly turbulent geopolitical context marked by democratic backsliding, growing Big Tech dependencies, and urgency around digital sovereignty. This symposium seeks to explore how we should think about digital government and the provision of public services in light of these shifting technological and political conditions.

Keynote Session One
Paolo Gerbaudo
The Platform State: Digital Sovereignty and the Return of Industrial Policy

This presentation challenges the popular idea that in the digital age the state is weakening in favour of multinational corporations, arguing instead that platformization leads to a profound reinvigoration of state power, particularly for those states such as the US that have control over their own technological ecosystem.

As governments embed reprogrammable data infrastructures into their core institutions, they gain new capacities for algorithmic legibility and behavioral steerability. This mode of control intermixes elements of governmentality and more coercive logics of the state, enabling a pervasive yet often invisible statecraft where the state re-integrates governance functions.

Drawing examples from Palantir and the use of artificial intelligence by ICE, the presentation explores the implications of this emerging paradigm for the future of power, citizenship, and our understanding of the nature of the state in the digital era.

Keynote Session Two
Dang Nguyen
Rendering Domination Technical: Gender and the Hardening of Digital Sovereignty

Statecraft, Sovereignty and Digital Government are intensely gendered projects. They are organised around masculinised fantasies of command, foresight, and control that are materially embedded in technical systems and administrative routines.

Digital governance does not simply modernise the state — it hardens particular sovereign dispositions by automating judgement, distancing responsibility, and reconfiguring care as compliance. Gender makes visible how authority is performed and protected in these systems, especially through claims of neutrality, efficiency, and inevitability.

This paper contends that contemporary digital statecraft functions as a technology of power that consolidates sovereignty while disavowing the violence and exclusions it produces. The paper closes by considering how attention to gender unsettles the apparent coherence of digital sovereignty, opening it to critique, friction, and re-composition.

Keynote Session Two
Fenwick McKelvey
The New Technologies of Freedom: Mainframes, PCs, and AI

Technologies co-create sensibilities of political freedom. Drawing on research from the forthcoming book SimPolitics, this presentation explores how political freedoms have been expressed through and with computational technologies.

Mainframe computers promoted the development of privacy law and personal liberties. The personal computer was articulated as a general-purpose technology whose social worth hinged on networked individualism. Minicomputers helped rearticulate a micropolitical collectivism, leaping from file sharing to Kony 2012 and late social media.

Artificial intelligence is a massive privacy violation and a negation of decades of networked peer production, but also ascendent and mundane. Through historical reflection, this presentation questions the emerging mode of political freedoms in an age of AI.

Keynote Session One
Cecilia Rikap
Digital sovereignty crossroads

The presence of Big Tech leaders in the front row at Trump’s inauguration signals a ruling coalition capable of controlling nearly every organization and nation that becomes reliant on their AI and other digital technologies. This alliance between corporate and state superpowers is strategically positioned to exert global dominance — with the exception of a few decoupled spaces, notably China — by controlling the social production of information and knowledge in all its forms, from narrative-building in social media to advanced AI technologies for warfare and surveillance.

In Europe, responses have differed. In the United Kingdom, the tech deal signed with the US signals that the UK government is prioritizing the promise of digital-driven economic growth at the expense of sovereignty. The case of the European Union is equally crossed by the same trade-offs.

This presentation explores how the UK and the EU are responding to pressures from the US government and US Big Tech, considering trade-offs between: AI’s potential for economic growth, historical geopolitical alliances and rivalries, digital technologies’ socio-ecological footprint, Big Tech’s control of the AI value chain, and digital sovereignty.

§ 01

About

From early e-government experiments in the 1990s to today’s AI-driven public services, states have long sought to harness digital technologies to transform how they interact with citizens. The 2010s saw many countries collaborate on “digital transformation,” building an international community around ideals of openness and participation, while simultaneously competing in global rankings and using digital initiatives as instruments of nation branding and statecraft.

These dynamics unfold within a highly turbulent geopolitical context marked by democratic backsliding, growing Big Tech dependencies, and urgency around digital sovereignty. This symposium seeks to explore how we should think about digital government and the provision of public services in light of these shifting technological and political conditions.

Infrastructural Power & Politics I
Gavin Duffy
Digital governance made material: AI data centres and local communities The rise of AI in recent years, in both cultural and political imaginaries, has resulted in a great deal of discussion around the future of democracy and sovereignty, whether through the direct use of AI systems in governance (such as the UK government’s Red Box) or the more indirect but no less important influence of AI boosters in setting political agendas (Gebru and Torres, 2024). At the same time, there has been an increasing focus on the material infrastructure required to support these AI imaginaries, particularly data centres. This is seen again in policy (e.g. the UK government’s planned AI Growth Zones) and in academic with this literature focusing on how this infrastructure can be examined critically and resisted locally literature (e.g. Rone, 2024; Lehuedé, 2025). This raises questions around sovereignty for local communities and, in particular, for Indigenous populations in many countries whose land and water rights are often threatened by the construction of data centres. This presentation will detail critical AI data centre studies, as part of the findings of a scoping review of this field, focusing on empirical data collection and analysis. In turn, this paper will present empirical findings from the author on local resistance to data centres in London, asking what parallels can be drawn (if any) and what issues may be spatially specific. Thus, in contrast to larger policy debates focusing on sovereignty at the national or international levels (e.g. in the EU), this presentation will address the question of how what sovereignty means in an age of AI at a local, community level. This will present the need for a focus on the ‘everyday’ of AI, generating a non-digital-centric idea of digital governance using the framework of AI justice.

Infrastructural Power & Politics I
Bastian Manteuffel
Infrastructural politics in the digital administrative state: Municipal agency under fragmented governance conditions. The availability of digital administrative services is routinely mobilized as an indicator for modern state-ness (see European Commission, 2025; Barry, 2001). In Germany, efforts to transform internal state affairs by means of digital technologies have been characterized as both institutionally, and infrastructurally fragmented, with the lack of progress usually discussed as a governance problem of vertical, and horizontal coordination. In this view, administrative digitization is a heterogenous phenomenon contested also between state actors lacking articulation as on how it ought to be realized and what it should solve. This is particularly visible at the municipal level which delivers the majority of administrative services, yet is granted constitutional autonomy over how this is done. Rather than problematizing the implementation per se, this paper is interested in how the German case reveals how digital state-making is not a monolithic state activity but unfolds across institutional, and infrastructural spaces. While institutional conditions provide the backdrop of responsibilities, and intergovernmental communication, infrastructural elements function both as sites of contestation, where local demands, and autonomy are negotiated, and as modes of governing, either through technical standards, or the "installed base" (Aanestad et al., 2017). Drawing on an ongoing research project (2021-), this paper traces digital state-making through municipal digital building permits, digital planning, and an internal modernization project, showing how tensions on the infrastructural layer revolve around issues of translation, institutional shifts, and temporality. This paper looks at these phenomena in terms of infrastructural politics governed by uncertainty. While existing research has revealed the highly consequential politics of digital administrative practice (Peeters & Widlak, 2023; Abdu & Jacobs, 2024; Ranchordas, 2021), this paper emphasizes the mundane, often unfinished activities of digital infrastructuring. If indeed, future states increasingly appear as infrastructural stacks, where democratic, and bureaucratic values are displaced along wider sociotechnical networks, it seems, that these phenomena require scholarly attention even when their social consequences can not be immediately assessed.

Infrastructural Power & Politics I
Julia Valeska Schröder
While the ever more explicit etatization of Digital Transformation is intensified through high-tech “GovTech”, also analyses of digital statecraft tend to focus on digital technologies as transformative force of the state (e.g. Berg & Hofmann, 2021; Fourcade & Gordon, 2020; Katzenbach & Ulbricht, 2019; Wong, 2021). However, the simultaneous advancement of digital politics is crucial to understand the more-than-digital (Klausner, 2022) dimensions digital statecraft. This contribution emphasizes the role of institutionalized digital politics for governmental technologies of Digital Transformation: The enactment of crisis narratives, used to portray the state as being at risk and legitimizing new (re)forms and (infra)structures; the conceptualization of ‚digital sovereignty‘ as reinterpretation of state power in the digital constellation (Berg et al., 2020) to reclaim authority over socio-political developments; as well as the operationalization of technopolitical modes that specify the practices of transformation and thereby the relationships between technology and politics. Not least, digital political statecraft deconstructs “the state” as a structural effect (Abrams, 1988) by turning the constituent relational infrastructures and their social and processual character outward; enacting “the state” no longer solely in formal political registers, but as fragile, processual infrastructural achievement – thus visibilizing the material and socio-technical foundations of state power. Based on an ethnographic study of the Public Innovation Lab in Berlin (CityLAB), I develop the concept of ‘intrastatecraft’ to highlight a specific programmatic shift away from technocentric approaches. Intrastatecraft is characterized by techniques of craft-like ‘intraventions’ in infrastructural-administrative situations (Birkbak & Papazu, 2022). So-called „public changemakers“ act locally on the affective, “poetic” (Larkin, 2013) entanglements of administrative infrastructures. They experiment with their embedding within other (infra)structures, with the community of users, and with cultural conventions of use (Star, 1999) in order to participate in the infrastructuring power within the executive processes of the state The Prefix “intra” thus highlights the importance of understanding governmental technologies beyond Foucault (2009, 2022) as the interaction with the formal boundaries of political government and governing of the practitioners of state administration. Further, paying closer attention to the component craft, ‘intrastatecraft’ aims to sensitize for statecrafting – the situated, skillful, experimental and creative production of power relations.

Nika Mahnic
The indentured state: programmable infrastructures and the loss of infrastructural power The public/private boundary often limits theoretical insight, with calls for public values in publicly funded infrastructures (Taylor, 2021) falling short of the long-lasting enmeshment of the public and private. I address the enmeshment of the public and the private with reference to the ‘governmental sphere’, composed of public and private actors collaborating on the ways of governing the state (Weiss, 2019, pp. 6-15). In my PhD project, I analysed the contemporary relevance of the imaginaries of management consultants (de)forming the British state since the nineteenth century onwards — their role in state (de)formation since the birth of the British Civil Service. More recently, reading into digital transformation through Brett Christophers’s forms of rentierism (Christophers, 2022) the thesis illuminates the various forms of rent pursuant to the building of the British “programmable infrastructures” (Gürses et al., 2020). In that way, digital statecraft can be seen as a way of advancing the interests of the governmental sphere. Building on interdisciplinary secondary literature, grey literature and archival records, I expand Dencik’s concept of the “tenant state” (2022, p. 161), contributing the concept of ‘the indentured state.’ Analysing ‘the indentured state’ as a political formation and a condition enables us to clarify the consequences of transforming the state(s) digitally, beyond the flows of rent harvested by the governmental sphere. Referring to the state as a condition, ‘the indentured state’ refers to the state of being captive; to digital transformation, contracts, workfare and indebtedness. A historically-informed analysis of the British welfare state (Bhambra, 2021; Lewis, 2000), focused on a political economy of risk assessment, reveals how Others have been subjected to a double indenture, while some have been signing generous long-term infrastructure contracts.

Infrastructural Power & Politics II
Loup Cellard & Clément Marquet
How public-private relations shape the ordering of digital infrastructures? Between 2018 and 2024, the American company Digital Realty constructed four data centers at the Marseille-Fos Port, an industrial hub located on the northern coast of Marseille, France. The port seized the opportunity presented by the city’s rapidly growing status in global data exchanges. Indeed, over the past decade, Marseille has ascended from 44th to 5th place among the world’s most connected cities. This new status has garnered particular interest from the port's main shareholder, the French government, which aims to transform the site into a strategic asset for enhancing national digital sovereignty. In this context, we wonder how the (mis)alignment of interests between regional State agencies and private infrastructural actors shape the ordering of data centers and subsea telecom cables in Marseille (France)? We have identified three phases that illustrate the complex relationships between the State and private actors involved. Firstly, prior to 2021, the development of digital infrastructures in Marseille was not a matter for the state but for the private sector only. Digital Realty aimed at transforming Marseille into a key data exchange hub. This resulted in two things: the arrival of major clients such as Microsoft and Disney+; a strong financial investment in the construction of a connection gate facilitating the plugging within the industrial port of submarine cables into data centres. Secondly, between 2021 and 2023, local elected officials and state agencies began to contest the massive rise of data centres and submarine cables within the city. The prefecture (which represents the State at the regional level) brought the various stakeholders together to find compromises and facilitate the work of Digital Realty and other data centre operators. Here, the prefecture (and therefore the State) seem to be facilitating the work of the private sector — despite the fact that local elected officials never fully understood the State's sovereignty strategy. Thirdly, since 2024, within regional state agencies, the call to promote national sovereignty began to be productive but remain fragile: the way in which cables are laid has been redefined; the city's urban planning agency has produced recommendations to improve the urban integration of data centres. The case of Marseille shows how the alignment between the public and private sectors is never complete and that the national slogan of sovereignty is struggling to fully push local State actors to regulate infrastructural work. Ultimately, private players such as Digital Realty remain the main actor ordering the ways infrastructures are developed in the city.

Title: Sovereignty by design: AI gigafactories, public-private partnerships and Europe’s infrastructural ‘turn’ Abstract: Sovereignty by design: AI gigafactories, public-private partnerships and Europe’s infrastructural ‘turn’ Matilde Bro, PhD-student, University of Copenhagen, Department of Political Science [email protected] This article examines how digital sovereignty in the European Union (EU) is designed and co-produced by EU institutions, (member) states and private consortia’s through public private partnerships. Through a case study of the EU Commissions launch of the so-called AI gigafactories, a public-private partnerships initiated to establish supercomputing ecosystems across five of EU’s member states to store data, attract talent and develop advanced (European) AI models, I contribute with new insights to how the processes, relations and practices of building AI infrastructure design and co-produce digital sovereignty in Europe. Designing such a European alternative to American digital infrastructure, which European citizens, companies and governments are deeply dependent on embodies Europe’s infrastructural ‘turn’, positioning digital infrastructure as a geopolitical asset and a site of contestation between private and public actors over who should own and operate it. Drawing on Science & Technology studies (STS) and International Relations’ (IR) infrastructural ‘turn’, I conceptualize these AI gigafactories as public private partnerships of (geo)political infrastructures that (re)order space, authority and identity. I argue that EU’s AI gigafactories operate on two levels: material – embodying sovereignty in physical supercomputing capacity, certifications and standards; and narrative – seeking security-of-self for Europe as a technological actor amid global dependencies. Through these levels, I identity three sovereignty dilemmas of control, territorialization and membership. Empirically, the article draws on ethnographic fieldwork in Brussels, combining participant observation, semi-structured interviews with EU’s digital policymakers and industry actors as well as document analysis of official material (policy paper, statements, speeches) released by EU institutions, (member) states and companies between 2023 and 2025. The multi-sited approach reveals how infrastructural governance becomes a site of identity-making and geopolitical positioning contributing to the literature on sovereignty, security and sociotechnical imaginaries.

Infrastructural Power & Politics II
Dan M. Kotliar & Alex Gekker
Contested Clouds: Digital Sovereignty and the Politics of National Data Infrastructure in Israel/Palestine Recent scholarship has called for the ‘comprehensive democratisation of digital sovereignty’ (Rone 2023, 1), positioning citizen participation as central to equitable cloud infrastructure governance. Yet, this call presumes a shared understanding of what sovereignty means and whose ‘matters-of-concern’ (Latour 2004; 2005) data infrastructures should serve. This paper examines Israel's Project Nimbus, a $1.2 billion national initiative to “migrate the state” into GCP and AWS (Kotliar and Gekker 2024), to explore how digital sovereignty is contested, claimed, and refused by divergent publics (Thielmann and Borbach 2025). Drawing on discourse analysis of government documents, corporate communications, news press, and activist media, we trace three distinct contestations of what Nimbus is. First, we show how Google and Amazon worked to naturalise their data centres as benign matters of fact through techno-nationalist imagery, framing the cloud as operating "on Israel's terms" while strategically obscuring their global cloud empires. Second, we examine how Israeli residents and military figures reframed Nimbus through securitised imaginaries: not as an environmental threat (as in Dutch or Irish national cases), but as vulnerable national infrastructure exposed to missile attacks and cyber threats. Third, we analyse the #NoTechForApartheid campaign, which mobilised a transnational counterpublic around Nimbus as an instrument of occupation and human rights abuse. These contestations reveal digital sovereignty as a floating signifier (Glasze et al. 2023; Couture et al. 2024), claimed simultaneously bythe state (jurisdictional control), local residents (protection from external threats), and international activists (ethical accountability). Rather than converging on deliberative co-production, these publics talk past one another, their attachments to the cloud shaped by incommensurable concerns. The paper concludes by asking what democratic governance of cloud infrastructure can mean when the "shape of the cloud" itself is so thoroughly contested: as the same data centres appear as tools of globalised progress, threatened national assets, and shadows of occupation, depending on one's vantage point.

AI in the State, Expertise & Legitimacy I
Alexander Campolo
Action: AI-enabled Government in the UK. A literature on AI and government has recently made promises to identify “a new national purpose” (Blair and Hague 2023) or “a new model to transform the state” (Iosad et al., 2024). And in January 2025, the Labour government of the United Kingdom duly presented its “AI Opportunities Action Plan,” to Parliament (Clifford 2025). This presentation analyses the emerging political logic of such calls to “action” on AI. Despite nostalgic references to mid-twentieth century industrial policy and personal connection to the New Labour’s neoliberal wing, I argue that contemporary reflection on AI and government operates within a political context that has substantially transformed, marked by new geopolitical divisions and mercantilist rivalries. Rather than representing an irrationalist rejection of expertise in favour of culture war issues, AI makes a politicised “claim to techné”—a privileged competence for delivering what citizens, here theorised as a single, disaffected body of consumers of government services, desire (Bickerton and Invernizzi Accetti 2021, 31). This “technopopulist” reflection on AI and government further speculates on how AI will deliver services. Somewhat counter-intuitively, these proposals depart from a calculative, bureaucratic logic that has long linked computers and technocratic administration (Agar 2003). Nor do they represent, as some left-leaning scholars may hope, a renewed form of democratic planning (Durand et al. 2024; Benanav 2025). Above all, AI, in the form of digital government assistants, promises to radically disintermediate service provision, constituting a direct interface between the individual citizen and the state, now encoded into the parameters of machine learning models. Together, these transformations point to a shift in the relationship between politics and expertise. Displacing both bureaucratic inertia and neoliberal market failures, AI makes ambiguous political claims to renew social democracy through "delivery."

Becky Kazansky & Lina Dencik
Title: Tracing the co-production of AI demand in corporate and governmental discourses. Abstract: In the corporate language of their recent environmental sustainability reports, Big Tech companies characterise their current work to develop AI as one of duty to respond however possible to an overwhelming demand for AI ‘out there’ in the world. This duty, in turn, justifies reneging on responsibilities such as previous climate commitments, superfluous in any case, because AI is a primary solution to the climate crisis itself. The national AI strategies of countries working closely with these companies — such as the UK, the United States, and Canada — speak, on the other hand, of an obligation for governments to ‘keep up’ with AI developments. Keeping up means encouraging AI adoption across all sectors of society, whether through support for the buildout of new data centres and energy infrastructure, to the deepening integration of AI applications into public services and education. Amidst the spectre of an AI ‘bubble’ threatening to pop, this paper explores how corporate and governmental actors construct the idea of an unrelenting societal demand for AI which must be met through any means possible. The paper bases its findings on a discourse analysis of corporate sustainability reports, national AI strategy documents, and a tranche of government transparency documents demonstrating the scale of corporate lobbying by Big Tech companies. By analysing these documents together, the paper tracks how an agentic dance of duty and obligation serves to rationalise, naturalise, and justifiy current AI trajectories at national scales.

AI in the State, Expertise & Legitimacy I
Anuradha Sajjanhar
Artificial Intelligence is no longer a speculative frontier in UK governance but an operational reality across domains such as welfare, immigration, and health. While policy discourse often frames these technologies in techno-optimistic terms, promising efficiency, productivity, and more “human” services, their integration into the state is reshaping foundational questions of expertise, authority, and legitimacy. This paper develops the concept of AI as epistemic infrastructure to analyse these transformations. By epistemic infrastructure, I refer to the layered interplay of data practices, algorithmic systems, institutional routines, and discursive framings through which authority is stabilised and knowledge rendered actionable in governance. This approach shifts analytical focus from questions of algorithmic bias or technical performance to the broader epistemic conditions under which decisions are defined, justified, and legitimated. Empirically, the UK provides a critical case study. Positioned outside the EU AI Act, the UK has adopted a pro-innovation regulatory stance while simultaneously outsourcing technical capacity to private firms such as Palantir, Microsoft, and AWS. Preliminary findings from interviews and documentary analysis reveal a fragmented and opaque landscape in which AI systems are frequently introduced without settled frameworks of oversight, often uncovered only through Freedom of Information requests. The consequence is a redistribution of epistemic authority: civil servants’ discretion and professional judgement are increasingly subordinated to computational models and private technical expertise. The paper argues that this reconfiguration of expertise carries significant implications for democratic governance. AI systems not only mediate how policy problems are defined and prioritised, but also embed normative assumptions into administrative routines, narrowing the space for deliberation and contestation. By examining AI as epistemic infrastructure, I highlight the need to reconceptualise expertise as a site of political struggle, and to foreground accountability and democratic legitimacy in debates on digital governance.

Chiara Poletti
Digital statecraft from below? AI governance and democratic oversight in Welsh local government As debates on digital sovereignty intensify across Europe, much attention has focused on national strategies and supranational coordination (Falkner et al., 2024). Yet sub-national and devolved governments also navigate questions of technological dependency, public value, and democratic oversight. This contribution examines AI adoption in Welsh local government: a site where devolved governance, public service transformation, and participatory traditions intersect. Despite growing AI adoption in its public services, the UK lacks binding horizontal AI legislation, and public sector AI use continues to outpace regulatory clarity (Public Law Project, 2024). Legal safeguards are difficult to apply (Lazcoz & De Hert, 2023). Even "assistive" technologies adopted in public services have proved to change the ways in which the state operates, reshaping institutional workflows and professional judgement, embedding bias, and limiting transparency (Eubanks, 2018; Dencik et al., 2022). Wales offers a distinctive case: its governance is explicitly oriented toward public value through the Well-being of Future Generations (Wales) Act 2015, and the Welsh government’s Centre for Digital Public Services (CDPS) is developing significant co-production experiences in the digitalisation of public services. The contribution presents an initial mapping of AI-related initiatives across South Wales local authorities and a synthesis of co-production and participatory governance initiatives in Welsh public services. The Welsh case thus offers a vantage point for examining how digital statecraft operates at sub-national scales, and what forms of sovereignty and participation remain possible within nested governance arrangements.

While national statistical systems have historically served as core infrastructures of governance and citizenship, the growing relevance of big data, cloud computing, and AI-driven analytics increasingly challenges their autonomy and legitimacy. Digital sovereignty crossroads

The presence of Big Tech leaders in the front row at Trump’s inauguration signals a ruling coalition capable of controlling nearly every organization and nation that becomes reliant on their AI and other digital technologies. This alliance between corporate and state superpowers is strategically positioned to exert global dominance — with the exception of a few decoupled spaces, notably China — by controlling the social production of information and knowledge in all its forms, from narrative-building in social media to advanced AI technologies for warfare and surveillance.

In Europe, responses have differed. In the United Kingdom, the tech deal signed with the US signals that the UK government is prioritizing the promise of digital-driven economic growth at the expense of sovereignty. The case of the European Union is equally crossed by the same trade-offs.

This presentation explores how the UK and the EU are responding to pressures from the US government and US Big Tech, considering trade-offs between: AI’s potential for economic growth, historical geopolitical alliances and rivalries, digital technologies’ socio-ecological footprint, Big Tech’s control of the AI value chain, and digital sovereignty.

Keynote Session One
Paolo Gerbaudo
Self-Deport Apps: The Pervasive Control of the Platform State and the New Digital Imperialism

This presentation challenges the popular idea that in the digital age the state is weakening in favour of multinational corporations, arguing instead that platformization leads to a profound reinvigoration of state power, particularly for those states such as the US that have control over their own technological ecosystem.

As governments embed reprogrammable data infrastructures into their core institutions, they gain new capacities for algorithmic legibility and behavioral steerability. This mode of control intermixes elements of governmentality and more coercive logics of the state, enabling a pervasive yet often invisible statecraft where the state re-integrates governance functions.

Drawing examples from Palantir and the use of artificial intelligence by ICE, the presentation explores the implications of this emerging paradigm for the future of power, citizenship, and our understanding of the nature of the state in the digital era.

Keynote Session Two
Dang Nguyen
Rendering Domination Technical: Gender and the Hardening of Digital Sovereignty

Statecraft, Sovereignty and Digital Government are intensely gendered projects. They are organised around masculinised fantasies of command, foresight, and control that are materially embedded in technical systems and administrative routines.

Digital governance does not simply modernise the state — it hardens particular sovereign dispositions by automating judgement, distancing responsibility, and reconfiguring care as compliance. Gender makes visible how authority is performed and protected in these systems, especially through claims of neutrality, efficiency, and inevitability.

This paper contends that contemporary digital statecraft functions as a technology of power that consolidates sovereignty while disavowing the violence and exclusions it produces. The paper closes by considering how attention to gender unsettles the apparent coherence of digital sovereignty, opening it to critique, friction, and re-composition.

Keynote Session Two
Fenwick McKelvey
The New Technologies of Freedom: Mainframes, PCs, and AI

Technologies co-create sensibilities of political freedom. Drawing on research from the forthcoming book SimPolitics, this presentation explores how political freedoms have been expressed through and with computational technologies.

Mainframe computers promoted the development of privacy law and personal liberties. The personal computer was articulated as a general-purpose technology whose social worth hinged on networked individualism. Minicomputers helped rearticulate a micropolitical collectivism, leaping from file sharing to Kony 2012 and late social media.

Artificial intelligence is a massive privacy violation and a negation of decades of networked peer production, but also ascendent and mundane. Through historical reflection, this presentation questions the emerging mode of political freedoms in an age of AI.
